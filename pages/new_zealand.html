New Zealand Trip 2025

The pictures and descriptions only scratch the surface. At some point, when time permits, I’ll post a video on YouTube and add the link here.

After about 24 hours of travel, which included 4 flights, several car rides and just barely making our connections in time, due to a surprise snow storm in Denver, we landed in Queenstown, NZ. We then drove to Aoraki Mt Cook National Park. The three pictures above show some of the scenery as we approached. Although we arrived during summer, there was plenty of snow on the mountains. We passed the Clutha River. Like many of the rivers and lakes in NZ, the water is a stunning blue color. This is due to glacial melt carrying silt, or glacial flour, as its sometimes called. The minerals in the silt scatter blue light.

We arrived at the Hermitage Hotel at the foot of Mt Cook - well - its actually 9 miles away but it feels like its right on top of you. As you can see, there is plenty of hiking to be had and avalanches are a real concern at certain times of year. We could see areas where huge masses of snow had come down and left shear snow cliffs behind.

The hotel has undergone many iterations, dating back to 1884. The views from our, and most rooms are breathtaking. There is a seasonal menu restaurant called Alpine on site where you can enjoy an amazing meal as you watch the sun set. A life size bronze statue honoring Edmund Cook sits out front.

We did a short hike. I wanted to do more but upon arrival I was not feeling well and had to go to a clinic for some antibiotics. I made a follow up visit a couple days later. I’ll just interject that the medical care was great and reasonably priced. Anyway, determined to enjoy whatever I could and not dwell on feeling under the weather, we did what ever my body would allow. As you can see, the scenery was spectacular. Being something of a botany geek, I was in heaven with so many new (to me) plants to explore.

I don’t know what I can say here that the pictures don’t already express. These shots were taken near the town of Twizel. The lake covers 69 square miles and the outflow into the Pulaski River is used to generate hydroelectric power.

We found a place where we could get right up to the water. If I hadn’t taken these photos myself, I would have been sure they were Photo-shopped. They are truly unedited pictures taken with my iPhone12 mini.

There are some dark skies areas and observatories above Lake Tekapo. We only had the opportunity to see it during the day, but again, the views are amazing and there’s a great cafe at the top of the mountain.

Patiti Point is about halfway down the eastern shore and was our first opportunity (other than from an airplane) to see the ocean. Later, we saw this rainbow as we passed through Omarama.

The town of Wanaka is really cool. The tree in the first picture is called the Wanaka tree and its almost obligatory to be photographed in front of it. I found the emergency beer truck amusing and had to take a picture. There is a park by the water with sculptures and paths. The town itself is filled with good restaurants and cafes.

As you wander around the lake you’ll come across beautiful gardens. You can continue walking on trails for about 20 miles that take you to various points of interest and camp grounds. Wanaka is also the location of my follow up medical visit. Great facility. It took the fear out of being sick half way around the world.

I was feeling pretty good when we stopped at Pisa Conservation Area and we did an impromptu hike into the mountains. These were some of the same mountains we flew over when we landed in Queenstown. When we got up high enough, we were actually looking down at planes and helicopters entering and exiting the airport.

I geeked it up again in Pisa when I discovered more alpine plants. I was really excited to discover Mountain Snow Berries (Gaultheria depressa) which are edible.(see center photo) Depressa, in the latin name means low-lying, which is a trait of many alpine plants. Mild in flavor but tasting similar to a blue berry - at least to my palate.

I had been complaining that in spite of how many sheep we had seen, I didn’t have a chance to get any good pictures - and then this happened. We stopped as they crossed the road. The owner of the heard came to the car and told us to drive slowly towards them and they would move…so we did and like the Red Sea, they did in fact, part. At the back end of the heard was a guy on a four wheel and two sheep dogs doing there thing.

Fiordland National Park has an inland section full of lakes that abruptly stop and transition into a woodland, full of ferns, mushrooms, mosses and epiphytes. A ribbon of course sand and pebbles serves as the boundary between the woods and lakes.

A several hours drive takes you to the ocean side of the fiords. There are numerous points you can visit, but we chose Milford Sound, part of the Unesco World Heritage site called Te Wahipounamu. Otherworldly, with mountains, cliffs and waterfalls that all seem too big and beautiful to be real.

We drove back to Queenstown and spent our last night there before flying back to the US.
